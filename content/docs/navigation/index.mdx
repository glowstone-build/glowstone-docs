Glowstone uses an **orbit camera** that revolves around a target point in world space, with dolly and pan controls. Drive it directly with the mouse, or snap straight to a preset view with the on-screen navigation gizmo, a radial pie menu, or the numpad.

## Mouse and trackpad

The viewport reads direct pointer input. Drag to orbit, hold `Shift` to pan, and scroll to zoom toward whatever is under the cursor.

| Action | Input | Behaviour |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Orbit | `Drag` (no modifier) | Rotate the camera around the target; the scene follows your hand |
| Pan | `Shift`+`Drag` | Slide the target across the view plane, keeping distance constant |
| Zoom (dolly) | `Scroll` | Change distance; zooms toward the world point under the cursor |
| Place 3D cursor | `Shift`+`Right-click` | Drop the 3D cursor (the transform pivot) on the surface under the pointer |
| Select | `Click` | Select the object under the pointer |
| Add to selection | `Cmd`/`Ctrl`+`Click` | Toggle an object in or out of the current selection |

<Callout type="info" title="Zoom-to-cursor">
Scrolling zooms toward the world point beneath the pointer, so the surface under the cursor stays fixed on screen. If the cursor is pointing at the sky, it falls back to the ground plane (`y=0`).
</Callout>

<Callout type="idea">
Pan speed scales with distance, so panning feels consistent whether you are framing the whole rig or inspecting a single gobo. Zoom in before you pan for fine adjustments.
</Callout>

## The camera model

The camera is fully described by a target point, two orbit angles, a distance, and a projection. The orbit pose (target, angles, distance, FOV, clip planes) is saved with the project file (`.glow`); [camera bookmarks](/docs/navigation/bookmarks) additionally store the projection flag per slot. The projection toggle itself is session state and resets to perspective on load.

    pitch: { description: 'Vertical angle, clamped to ±1.5533 rad (~±89°) to avoid flipping over the poles.', type: 'radians', default: '0.08' },
    distance: { description: 'Dolly distance from the target to the camera eye. Clamped to 0.5–200 m.', type: 'm', default: '7.5' },
    fov_y: { description: 'Vertical field of view (perspective mode).', type: 'degrees', default: '55°' },
    znear: { description: 'Near clipping plane.', type: 'm', default: '0.05' },
    zfar: { description: 'Far clipping plane.', type: 'm', default: '500.0' },
    ortho: { description: 'Projection mode: false = perspective, true = orthographic. Session state (not persisted in the project file).', type: 'bool', default: 'false' },
  }}
/>

<Callout type="info">
The default view frames a typical fixture and beam: target at roughly 2.7 m height, a 3/4 yaw of about 34° (0.6 rad), a gentle 4.6° pitch (0.08 rad), and a distance of 7.5 m.
</Callout>

## Preset views

Six axis views plus a default perspective view frame the scene from a fixed direction. Selecting an axis view (Front, Back, Left, Right, Top, Bottom) switches the camera to **orthographic**; selecting Perspective restores it. Every transition eases over roughly a quarter-second (`0.25 s`), taking the shortest arc.

| View | What you see | Projection on load |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Front | Eye on +Z, looking down −Z | Ortho |
| Back | Eye on −Z, looking down +Z | Ortho |
| Left | Eye on −X, looking down +X | Ortho |
| Right | Eye on +X, looking down −X | Ortho |
| Top | Overhead bird's-eye, looking down | Ortho |
| Bottom | Floor view, looking up | Ortho |
| Perspective | Default 3/4 view; restores perspective | Persp |

## Perspective vs orthographic

Glowstone renders in **perspective** by default. Switch to **orthographic** for parallel, plan-style views when checking alignment and symmetry.

<Tabs items={['Perspective', 'Orthographic']}>
  <Tab value="Perspective">
    The natural 3/4 working view. Objects converge toward vanishing points, matching how the rig reads on stage. Vertical FOV defaults to `55°` and is saved per bookmark.
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Orthographic">
    Parallel projection with no perspective divide. The frame size is matched to the perspective framing at the same distance, and clipping is symmetric about the target so geometry between the eye and target is never front-clipped.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

Toggle between the two with `Numpad 5`, or via the **Persp/Ortho** entry in the [View pie](#pie-menus). Switching the projection animates smoothly over `0.25 s`.

## Navigation gizmo

The navigation gizmo is the cluster of six coloured balls in the top-right corner of the viewport. It rotates live with the camera, doubling as an orientation readout, and you click a ball to snap the view to that axis.

<ImagePlaceholder
  label="Close-up"
  ratio="1 / 1"
  caption="Close-up of the navigation gizmo: six axis balls arranged as a 3D sphere. The +X (red), +Y (green) and +Z (blue) balls are solid and labelled X, Y, Z; the negative −X, −Y, −Z balls are hollow rings. A faint backing disc sits behind the cluster."
/>

### Reading the gizmo

- **Colours** follow the standard convention: X is red, Y is green, Z is blue.
- **Positive balls** (+X, +Y, +Z) are solid circles labelled with their axis letter.
- **Negative balls** (−X, −Y, −Z) are hollow rings with no label, so the cluster reads as a 3D sphere.
- Depth drives draw order — near balls overlap far ones — and each ball's arm fades when it points away from you. Hovering a ball draws it solid and highlights it in white.

### Clicking a ball

Click a ball to ease the camera to look straight down that axis:

| Ball | Snaps to |
| --- | --- |
| +X (red, solid) | Right view |
| −X (red, hollow) | Left view |
| +Y (green, solid) | Top view |
| −Y (green, hollow) | Bottom view |
| +Z (blue, solid) | Front view |
| −Z (blue, hollow) | Back view |

<Callout type="idea">
When near and far balls overlap on screen, the nearer (front-facing) ball wins the click — so you always grab the ball facing you.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="Hide the gizmo">
The navigation gizmo is on by default. Toggle it from **Preferences → Viewport → Navigation gizmo**, the **View menu → Overlays**, or the bound `view.toggle_gizmos` command. While hidden it is neither drawn nor clickable.
</Callout>

## Pie menus

Pie menus are cursor-anchored radial menus. Two cover camera and display work: the **View pie** and the **Shading pie**. Both open at the cursor and pick instantly — no confirmation step.

<Tabs items={['View pie', 'Shading pie']}>
<Tab value="View pie">

Press `` ` `` (backtick / tilde) to open the View pie. Its eight sectors gather every preset view plus the projection toggle and frame-selection:

- Top view
- Right view
- Persp/Ortho toggle
- Back view
- Bottom view
- Frame Sel. (eased fit-to-view)
- Front view
- Left view

</Tab>
<Tab value="Shading pie">

Press `Z` to open the Shading pie. It combines viewport display modes with quick overlay toggles, applied immediately on pick:

- **Display modes:** Beauty, Unlit, Wireframe
- **Overlay toggles:** Grid, Stats

For the full set of display modes and overlays, see [Display Modes & Overlays](/docs/navigation/overlays).

</Tab>
</Tabs>

### Picking from a pie

<Steps>
<Step>
### Open the pie
Press the menu key (`` ` `` for View, `Z` for Shading). The pie appears centred on the cursor over a dimmed backdrop.
</Step>
<Step>
### Aim at a sector
Move the cursor toward the sector you want. The hovered sector's pill fills with the accent colour and a pointer line follows the cursor.
</Step>
<Step>
### Pick
Either click the sector, or use a press-drag-release gesture from the centre outward and release on the sector.
</Step>
<Step>
### Cancel if needed
Release inside the central dead-zone disc, or press `Esc`, to dismiss the pie without choosing anything.
</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="info">
Pie shortcuts (and all others) are rebindable. Open **Preferences → Keymap**, click a command's shortcut chip, and press a new chord. Custom binds persist to `keymap.json` immediately.
</Callout>

## Numpad

The numpad gives fast keyboard access to the same preset views, the projection toggle, and fixed-step orbiting. These binds live in the **Viewport** keymap, so they fire only while the 3D viewport is focused. `Cmd`/`Ctrl` flips an axis view to its opposite-facing pair.

| Shortcut | Action |
| --- | --- |
| `Num1` | Front view |
| `Cmd`/`Ctrl`+`Num1` | Back view |
| `Num3` | Right view |
| `Cmd`/`Ctrl`+`Num3` | Left view |
| `Num7` | Top view |
| `Cmd`/`Ctrl`+`Num7` | Bottom view |
| `Num5` | Toggle perspective / orthographic |
| `Num8` / `Num2` | Orbit up / down (pitch) |
| `Num4` / `Num6` | Orbit left / right (yaw) |

<Callout type="info">
egui maps the numpad digits onto the same key codes as the top-row digits, so these binds fire on either row. Like every shortcut, they are rebindable in **Preferences → Keymap**.
</Callout>

<Callout type="idea">
The orbit commands (`view.orbit_up` / `down` / `left` / `right`) rotate the camera by a fixed 15° step, so you can spin around a fixture in even increments without dragging.
</Callout>

## Framing the scene

Framing fits the view to your selection or the whole scene, independent of the view angle. Both operations ease the camera to centre and fit the target over `0.25 s`, recomputing the distance to fit the bounding box.

| Command | Default key | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Frame Selection | `F` (or `.`) | Aim at and fit the selected objects |
| Frame All | `Shift`+`F` (or `Home`) | Aim at and fit the entire scene |

<Steps>
<Step>
### Select what to frame
`Click` a fixture or object in the viewport. For Frame All, no selection is needed.
</Step>
<Step>
### Frame the selection
Press `F` to aim at and fit the current selection. The camera dollies so the selection fills the view with a small margin.
</Step>
<Step>
### Frame everything
Press `Shift`+`F` to fit the entire scene in view.
</Step>
</Steps>

The [View pie](#pie-menus) also includes a **Frame Selection** sector.

<Callout type="idea">
Grabbing the camera at any time — orbit, pan or zoom — instantly cancels an in-flight framing or recall animation..
</Callout>

## The view tag

A small monospace label in the top-left corner of the viewport reports the current state in the form `<Projection> · <View name>`.

- Projection reads `Persp` or `Ortho`.
- The view name shows `Front`, `Back`, `Left`, `Right`, `Top` or `Bottom` when the orbit angles snap to a canned view; otherwise it reads `User` for a free orbit.

Examples: `Ortho · Front`, `Persp · User`.
